Parliamentary Decision Latency and Market Signal Correlation

Bond Spreads as Necessary but Insufficient Indicators of State Sustainability
Abstract. This paper examines whether financial market signals — specifically sovereign bond yield spreads — can detect parliamentary decision latency (D-suppression) before it manifests in fiscal indicators. Using Finland as a case study and the energy strategy review VNS 8/2025 vp as a parliamentary event anchor, we collect FI-DE and FI-SE bond spread data (Eurostat, 2023–2026) and code D-phase transitions. The pilot analysis reveals a null finding: FI-DE spread narrows monotonically from 0.60 pp (2023) to 0.40 pp (2025–2026), and FI-SE spread is dominated by Riksbank/ECB monetary policy divergence. We argue this null finding is theoretically significant: bond spreads are structurally incapable of detecting capacity investment deficits because credit rating models are backward-looking and optimise short-term fiscal consolidation. This is not a Finland-specific observation — it is an OECD-level correlated blindspot produced by three synchronisation mechanisms: financial architecture, institutional standards, and risk pricing models. The paper concludes with a reframed hypothesis: the relevant question is not whether markets anticipate D-suppression, but which instrument detects it first and with what lag. The asymmetry is the core finding: S&P reacted to Finland's fiscal framework review in under 24 hours; the same agency's model cannot detect an energy capacity investment deficit until it manifests in GDP or fiscal aggregates — a 5–10 year lag. As S&P's own April 2026 statement illustrates: energy prices are visible to rating models; energy capacity is not.
Pre-print notice. This paper is published in pre-print form to invite referee comments. The empirical pilot uses manually coded parliamentary event dates and Eurostat monthly yield data. The hypothesis has been revised once following null findings. Core thesis: bond spreads show no evidence of detecting capacity investment deficits in OECD AAA-tier economies within the observed window — consistent with, but not proof of, a structural limitation arising from the backward-looking design of credit rating models. § 01

Research Question and Motivation

The Aether Continuity Institute has documented a structural phenomenon in Finnish institutional decision-making: D-suppression, in which identified risks pass through a three-stage filter (D1: signal → report; D2: report → agenda; D3: agenda → decision) with significant delays at each transition. The energy capacity gap documented in the Finnish energy system structural report (ACI, 2026) provides a concrete case: the risk has been identified, reported, and placed on parliamentary agenda, but no legislative action has followed.

The research question of WP-017 is: Do financial markets price D-suppression — the failure of identified risks to become binding decisions — before it manifests in fiscal indicators?

This is motivated by a governance accountability argument. If markets detect decision-making capacity gaps with a measurable lead time, this creates a transparent, politically neutral signal that institutional actors cannot plausibly deny. The phrase "we did not know" becomes empirically testable.

§ 02

Theoretical Framework: D-Suppression and Market Pricing

2.1 The D-suppression model

D-suppression describes a three-stage institutional filter through which policy signals are systematically attenuated. D1 converts external signals into reports (low attenuation); D2 converts reports into agenda items (moderate attenuation); D3 converts agenda items into binding decisions (high attenuation). The current Finnish energy strategy review (VNS 8/2025 vp) has cleared D1 and D2 but as of April 2026 has not produced a capacity mechanism decision — a classic D3 blockage.

2.2 Revised hypothesis

The original hypothesis — that markets anticipate D-suppression — has been revised following the pilot data. The revised hypothesis is: sovereign bond spreads delay D-suppression pricing, reacting to institutional decision-making gaps typically with a 2–5 year lag and only after the problem has manifested. The research question is reframed: which instrument detects D-suppression earliest, and what is the lead time?

§ 03

Pilot Data: FI-DE and FI-SE Spreads

3.1 Data source and methodology

Yield data: Eurostat dataset irt_lt_mcby_m (EMU convergence criterion series, 10-year maturity, monthly). Countries: Finland (FI), Germany (DE), Sweden (SE). Period: January 2023 – March 2026 (39 observations). D-phase events coded manually from parliamentary records (VNS 8/2025 vp käsittelytiedot, eduskunta.fi).

3.3 FI-DE spread results

The FI-DE spread narrows monotonically over the observation period: from 0.60 pp average in 2023 to 0.53 pp in 2024 and 0.40 pp in 2025. During the D1-D2 transition (October–December 2025) the spread narrows from 0.40 to 0.35 pp — the opposite direction from what the original hypothesis predicted. In early 2026 a minor widening occurs (+0.065 pp at D2/committee phase) but is within normal variation.

Spread distribution (39 observations, 2023–2026): FI-DE mean 0.505 pp, median 0.50 pp, range 0.35–0.65 pp (Q1: 0.42, Q3: 0.59). The distribution is approximately normal with no fat tails — the spread decline is a monotonic trend, not driven by outliers. Annual means: 2023: 0.60 pp · 2024: 0.53 pp · 2025: 0.40 pp · 2026-Q1: 0.43 pp. The 2026 minor reversal is within Q1-Q3 interquartile range and does not constitute a statistically distinct regime shift.

Identification note: FI-DE isolates common ECB monetary policy but does not fully control for all spread components. Residual confounds include: (1) liquidity premium — German Bunds are the eurozone safe-haven benchmark, carrying a structural demand premium; (2) issuance dynamics — Finland's smaller debt stock creates different supply/demand dynamics; (3) inflation expectation differentials — ECB policy is common but transmission differs. These are second-order relative to the Riksbank problem (§3.4) and are unlikely to reverse the null finding. A regression controlling for ECB policy rate and euro-area VIX would be required to formally separate these components — identified as a v1.0 extension.

Finding 3.1: FI-DE spread does not detect D-suppression. AAA-Finland is priced near Germany regardless of energy policy decision latency. The spread narrows as Finland's fiscal profile converges toward Germany within the eurozone common monetary framework.

3.4 FI-SE spread results and monetary policy confound

FI-SE spread shows substantially higher volatility: it widens dramatically in summer 2024 (0.43 → 0.88 pp) as the Riksbank cut rates aggressively while Finland followed the ECB. This monetary policy divergence dominates any D-suppression signal. The spread narrows during the D1-D2 phase (2025-Q4: 0.685 → 0.345 pp) but this coincides with Swedish rate normalisation, not Finnish parliamentary action.

Finding 3.2a (placebo test 2020–2021): FI-SE spread was negative throughout 2020–2021 (range −0.18 to −0.53 pp) — Finland's yield was lower than Sweden's. This is a clean monetary policy signal: Riksbank did not cut at the ECB's pace during COVID. The spread flipped positive in mid-2022 with the energy crisis and Riksbank normalisation. This placebo period confirms that FI-SE measures Riksbank/ECB divergence, not Finland-specific structural risk. It also validates the instrument's sensitivity: when a genuine regime difference exists (monetary policy divergence), the spread detects it clearly.
Finding 3.2: FI-SE spread is contaminated by monetary policy divergence (ECB vs. Riksbank). Finland's eurozone membership — ceding monetary policy to the ECB — makes FI-SE an impure instrument. FI-DE is methodologically cleaner but also shows no D-suppression signal.
§ 04

Why Bond Spreads Cannot Detect Capacity Investment Deficits

4.1 The backward-looking structure of rating models

Sovereign credit rating agencies (S&P, Fitch, Moody's, Scope) use five-pillar models assessing institutional quality, economic structure, external position, fiscal strength, and monetary flexibility. Each pillar is scored on realised historical data. Investment pipelines — infrastructure modernisation, energy capacity mechanisms, PtX development — are invisible to the model until they produce measurable GDP or fiscal impact, which may take 5–10 years.

S&P's April 2026 statement on Finland is instructive: the agency called for "front-loaded measures" to stabilise debt. Front-loaded means: cut now, do not promise future growth. This incentive structure is structurally opposed to long-term capacity investment.

4.2 The 2008 precedent

The 2008 financial crisis is the canonical empirical evidence of credit rating model blindness. Rating agencies assigned AAA to subprime CDOs using backward-looking correlation models that missed building capacity gaps. At the sovereign level, Iceland, Ireland, and Spain held AA or above immediately before the crisis — agencies did not detect the transfer of private debt to public balance sheets. Downgrades came rapidly after realisation, not before.

The structural analogy to Finland is instructive but imperfect — the analogy is structural, not empirical equivalence. The asset classes differ (CDO vs sovereign), the risk direction differs (private→public vs public→private), and the timeframe differs. What is analogous is the mechanism: backward-looking models rated stable what was structurally deteriorating. The inverted direction: in 2008, private debt transferred to the public balance sheet. In Finland's case, the risk runs in the opposite direction — public decision-making failure transfers its costs to the private sector (industrial competitiveness, household energy costs, municipal heating capacity) on a 5–10 year horizon that exceeds rating model forward visibility.

4.3 The OECD correlated blindspot

Finland's situation is not idiosyncratic. Three synchronisation mechanisms produce the same allocation blindspot across OECD economies simultaneously:

MechanismHow it operatesEffect on capacity investment
Financial architecturePension funds, infrastructure funds, capital markets operate globally with short-term yield optimisationLong-horizon capacity investment does not fit return models
Institutional standardsOECD fiscal rules, EU Stability and Growth Pact, EDP procedure optimise short-term budget balanceCategory II investments (future capacity) penalised vs. Category I (current expenditure reduction)
Risk pricing modelsS&P, Fitch, Scope, Moody's use identical five-pillar frameworks globallySame blindspot simultaneously in all OECD member states

This is consistent with ACI's earlier RQM-001 finding (Transnational Allocation Template, WP-008/WP-009 context): allocation bias is not random across OECD economies but correlated through shared institutional architecture.

4.4 ECB as institutional cushion

The ECB's mandate is price stability — not investment capacity or structural resilience. Its single eurozone rate applies equally to Finland (which has not decided on a capacity mechanism) and France (which has legislated one). This creates an institutional cushion that hides national decision-making gaps in bond pricing. Sweden, with its independent Riksbank, has no equivalent cushion — but its monetary independence contaminates spread analysis as shown in §3.4.

The S&P action of 24 April 2026 provides a methodologically significant real-time observation. The rating change was recorded less than 24 hours after Finland's government framework spending review (kehysriihi). S&P's stated rationale: "persistent risks to public finances stemming from low growth, ageing demographics, and rising defence and interest expenditure" with a risk that "public debt may continue to rise over 2026–2029." Energy is mentioned only as a contextual factor: policymakers must "adapt to the impact that the crisis in the Middle East is having on energy prices." This confirms the fiscal dominance of the rating model: energy appears as a price risk, not a structural capacity risk.

The speed of the S&P reaction — sub-24-hour response to a fiscal event — demonstrates that rating agencies track short-term fiscal signals with high sensitivity. This is the inverse of the structural blindspot: when the signal is fiscal and immediate, the reaction is fast. When the signal is structural and long-horizon (capacity investment deficit), the reaction is slow or absent. This asymmetry is a core empirical finding of WP-017.

Finland's Finance Minister Purra acknowledged the action was anticipated: "Unfortunately, Finland's figures have made it quite clear that this was coming." This is a D3-suppression signal at the fiscal level — the problem was known, the decision was delayed, and the market signal arrived before corrective action was taken.

The typical timeline from negative outlook to downgrade is 6–18 months. If S&P follows Fitch's pattern, a downgrade decision at the October 2026 review would coincide with the opening of Finland's identified energy system convergence window (2027–2030). The causal driver would be fiscal — not energy capacity — but the interaction effect is material: a rating downgrade increases borrowing costs precisely when capacity investment need is highest.

Finding 5.1 (Asymmetric incentive structure — hypothesis): The rating agency framework rewards front-loaded fiscal consolidation and does not positively weight long-term capacity investment pipelines. This creates an asymmetric incentive: a government that cuts Category II investments (infrastructure, energy capacity) improves its short-term fiscal profile and may stabilise its rating outlook, while a government that maintains or increases such investments faces downgrade pressure even if the investments would improve long-term debt-carrying capacity. This is the proposed Salazar mechanism — named after the pattern in which rating pressure penalises exactly the investments needed to address the structural risk driving the downgrade.

Status: This is a structural hypothesis, not yet an empirical finding. The mechanism is theoretically coherent and consistent with the academic literature cited in §06 (UN DESA 2023, ScienceDirect 2025). Empirical validation would require a cross-country regression of rating outcomes against investment composition — beyond the scope of this pre-print. WP-017 names and frames the mechanism; it does not claim to have proven it.

Falsification criteria (referee response): The Salazar mechanism is falsified if: (A) Content analysis of 10–20 rating actions on Finland and 2–3 comparator countries finds that investment pipeline absence is cited as an independent negative factor — not merely as part of general fiscal deterioration. If rating statements never reference capacity investment deficits independently, the mechanism may operate implicitly rather than being absent. (B) Quantitative: a cross-country panel regression finds no negative correlation between Category II investment levels and rating outcomes after controlling for deficit and debt-to-GDP. Criterion A is feasible with available public rating statements and constitutes the immediate next empirical step for WP-017 v0.3.
§ 06

Academic Context

The structural blindness of credit rating models to long-term sustainability investment is an academically recognised problem. The UN Department of Economic and Social Affairs (2023) states that investments in resilience and climate adaptation can materially enhance a country's future debt-carrying capacity even when they increase short-term debt, and notes that rating methodology updates have been slow and typically reactive to past crises.

Recent empirical work (ScienceDirect, 2025) demonstrates that sovereign downgrades do not merely reduce aggregate investment — they reallocate capital from long-term to short-term projects. This is an empirical confirmation of the mechanism: rating pressure forces investment horizon shortening precisely when long-term capacity investment is most needed.

Moody's own methodology (2022) acknowledges environmental shocks and demographic change as growth determinants but retains operational weight on fiscal strength and short-term resilience. Long-term capacity investment is not a standalone positive variable in current agency frameworks.

§ 07

Conclusions and Next Steps

The pilot analysis produces a null finding on the original hypothesis: bond spreads do not detect D-suppression in the VNS 8/2025 vp case. This null finding is theoretically significant. It establishes that bond spreads — the most liquid and continuously priced sovereign risk indicator — are structurally incapable of signalling capacity investment deficits in OECD AAA-tier economies.

The reframed research agenda asks: which instrument detects D-suppression earliest? Candidates for the next phase of WP-017 analysis:

FI-DK preliminary finding: Denmark (ERM II — de facto euro peg, independent central bank within narrow band) provides a structurally interesting comparator. FI-DK spread was near-zero in 2020–2021 (0.06–0.29 pp) and has widened to 0.53–0.73 pp in 2025–2026. Unlike FI-SE, the FI-DK widening cannot be attributed to monetary policy divergence (Denmark shadows ECB closely). Denmark has pursued a more active energy capacity policy than Finland. If FI-DK widening reflects energy policy divergence rather than fiscal divergence, it would be the closest available signal to the D-suppression hypothesis. This is a tentative observation requiring fiscal decomposition before any causal claim can be made.

Cross-country validation (next phase): The OECD-level correlated blindspot argument requires validation beyond Finland. A three-country comparison is the minimum viable extension: Finland (eurozone, D-suppression identified), Sweden (own currency/Riksbank, comparable energy structure), Denmark (ERM II — de facto euro peg, different energy policy trajectory). Sweden controls for eurozone vs. Nordic effect; Denmark controls for energy policy decision-making differences within a similar monetary structure. Three countries suffice for pre-print extension; a full OECD panel (10–15 countries) would be required for a peer-reviewed journal submission.

Core conclusion: The pilot produces no evidence that bond spreads detect D-suppression within the observed 76-month window. This is consistent with, but does not prove, a structural limitation: rating models are backward-looking by design, and capacity investment deficits materialise on a horizon that exceeds their forward visibility. The absence of a signal in this pilot does not establish structural impossibility — it establishes that the signal, if present, lies below detection threshold in the instruments tested. This finding motivates the search for more sensitive instruments (§07).
Referee input requested on: (1) Is the monetary policy control methodology sufficient to isolate Finland-specific spread components? (2) Which alternative instrument is most tractable for next-phase analysis? (3) Is the 2008 structural analogy valid given the inverted risk direction? (4) Does the OECD correlated blindspot argument require additional cross-country validation?
