Working Paper — Labour Market Diagnostics

The Two Labour Market Paradox

Structural unemployment and labour shortage in Finland — with particular attention to workers aged 55 and above

Domains D-3 · D-4
Companion WP-008 · DA-006
Language EN (translated from FI)
Status Internal draft — number TBD
Abstract

Finland's labour market exhibits two apparently contradictory phenomena simultaneously: elevated unemployment and persistent labour shortage. This paper examines the phenomenon through the hypothesis that the labour market has divided into two partially separate segments, between which mobility is institutionally and organisationally constrained.

Particular attention is given to workers aged 55 and above, whose employment rates lag significantly behind those of the core workforce despite high levels of accumulated experience and skill. The paper argues that the central problem is not a deficit of available positions but an institutional matching failure — and that this failure has a structural character that is not resolved by standard demand-side interventions.

The analysis also examines the cultural dimension of contemporary work and its contribution to labour market segmentation.

§ 01

Introduction

Finland's labour market is characterised by a paradox: unemployment and labour shortage coexist. This is not exclusively a Finnish phenomenon, but its effects are particularly visible in Finland and have recurred persistently in both policy debate and research literature.

Labour shortage is especially acute in social and healthcare services, industrial and technical occupations, and the ICT sector. Simultaneously, long-term unemployment has remained a significant structural feature. This paper examines the phenomenon through a structural and institutional lens.

Research Question

How does the structural segmentation of the labour market explain the simultaneous occurrence of unemployment and labour shortage in Finland, with particular reference to workers aged 55 and above?

Method

This paper is based on conceptual analysis, statistical background data, and a case illustration. The approach is theory-guided: empirical material serves to illuminate the hypothesis rather than to test it statistically. A subsequent version will incorporate a systematic literature review and quantitative analysis.

The paper is structured as follows. Section 2 presents the theoretical framework. Section 3 provides a quantitative survey of the statistical evidence. Section 4 presents the segmentation diagram. Sections 5 and 6 address the specific situation of workers aged 55 and above and the role of network-based labour markets. Sections 7 and 8 examine institutional barriers and international comparisons. Sections 9, 10, and 11 cover the policy framework, the cultural dimension of work, and the implications for ageing societies. Section 12 draws conclusions.

§ 02

The Two Labour Market Hypothesis

Labour markets can be described through two partially separate segments. The classical economic model assumes that labour shortage and unemployment are corrected by the market mechanism. This paper argues that this corrective mechanism operates only partially in Finland.

Hypothesis Specification

The two labour market hypothesis does not posit entirely separate labour markets but a segmented structure in which movement between segments is institutionally and organisationally constrained. The segments are partially distinct — not absolutely isolated.

Labour Market A
High-skill positions · Labour shortage
  • High productivity
  • Persistent unfilled demand
  • Active formal recruitment
Labour Market B
Long-term unemployment · Exclusion risk
  • Overrepresentation of older workers
  • Partial work capacity
  • Weaker institutional integration
↕   Limited mobility between segments

Segmentation is produced by several simultaneous factors: skill matching failures, recruitment practices, institutional incentive structures, and informal networks that systematically favour certain groups. Labour shortage therefore does not automatically reduce unemployment — this is the core of the paradox.

§ 03

Quantitative Survey

This section presents the statistical background supporting the hypothesis. Data is drawn from Statistics Finland's Labour Force Survey (2023–2024), Eurostat age-group data, and EURES reports.

3.1 — Employment Rate by Age Group

The employment rate varies significantly across age groups. The position of the 55–64 age bracket is especially notable: their employment rate is 13.6 percentage points below that of the 35–54 core workforce, despite concurrent labour shortages in sectors where experienced workers are most valuable.

A positive development is visible among 60–64-year-olds: their labour force participation rose from 43% (2010) to 71% (2024), a major structural shift attributable in large part to pension reform (2005 and 2017). This illustrates that institutional change can materially affect the labour market position of older workers.

3.2 — Unemployment Rate by Age Group

In 2024 overall unemployment stood at 8.4% (ages 15–74), a 1.2 percentage point increase year-on-year. Long-term unemployment is projected to reach approximately 94,000 individuals in 2025. The age distribution reveals two concerning polarisation trends.

The quantitative paradox is clear: the 55–64 age group has an unemployment rate (8.6%) near the overall average while its employment rate (71.7%) sits well below that of the core workforce (85.3%). The explanation does not lie in willingness or capability — it lies in structure.

Quantitative Summary
  1. The employment rate of 55–64-year-olds (71.7%) is 13.6 percentage points below the core workforce — despite this group holding the highest accumulated experience.
  2. Labour shortage sectors (healthcare, ICT, construction) are precisely those in which older-worker experience is most valuable.
  3. Up to 47.8% of workers in certain sectors are aged 55–74 — they will exit the labour force within a decade with insufficient replacement cohorts.

§ 05

Workers Aged 55 and Above

The position of older workers in the labour market represents a particularly clear instance of the segmentation mechanism. Two forces converge in this group: deep accumulated professional competence on one hand, and structural employment barriers on the other.

Structural strengths: long professional experience and deep occupational knowledge; stable work motivation and organisational commitment; extensive professional networks.

Employment barriers: risk perceptions on the employer side associated with recruitment of unfamiliar older candidates; age-related preconceptions in hiring decisions; extended unemployment spells that compound over time and progressively reduce employment prospects.

Case Illustration — Experienced engineer, aged 58

A mechanical engineer with 30 years of process industry experience lost his position in 2022 when his employer ceased operations and relocated production abroad. Forty formal applications produced two interview invitations.

Employment ultimately came through an informal network: a former colleague recommended him to a subcontractor that needed precisely his skill profile. The engagement began as a three-month consulting contract and became permanent after six months.

This case is not an isolated exception — it illustrates the mechanism by which network-based recruitment functions as an alternative labour market operating in parallel to the formal open application system. The mechanism is structural, and its institutional reinforcement could materially improve employment outcomes for workers aged 55 and above.

The case illustrates a broader structural problem: experienced workers can remain outside the labour market while labour shortages persist simultaneously. This is a concrete instantiation of the gap between the two segments.

§ 06

Network Economy and the Hidden Labour Market

A significant share of positions is filled through informal networks. Open vacancy advertising constitutes only part of the labour market: research suggests that 50–70% of positions are filled without a public announcement (European Commission, 2023).

A typical network-based employment path proceeds through: an educational or prior employment relationship; contact with a firm through a personal network; a project or consulting engagement; and eventual permanent employment.

Educational institutions can function as important bridge-builders. Institutional mechanisms supporting network-based employment are currently underutilised. For workers aged 55 and above, networks are especially significant because formal application processes systematically produce worse outcomes for this group.

§ 07

Institutional and Cultural Barriers

Several interrelated factors contribute to the matching failure.

Recruitment risk perception. Employers may perceive hiring as high-risk. Dismissal protection and wage costs can reduce willingness to hire unfamiliar workers. This risk perception is heightened for older applicants even though the empirical evidence for elevated actual risk is weak.

Age-related preconceptions. Preconceptions associated with age or extended unemployment can influence recruitment decisions independently of actual job performance capacity (Böckerman & Ilmakunnas, 2012). This has been documented across multiple international studies.

Effectiveness of support instruments. In some cases employment subsidies do not lead to durable employment. The structure of subsidies does not always create incentives for long-term contracts — producing what is termed a gateway effect, where subsidy-supported positions function as temporary holding patterns rather than pathways to stable employment.

Digital skills and continuous learning. Rapid technological change requires continuous skill updating. Training opportunities do not always reach older workers adequately, although their learning capacity is not meaningfully diminished relative to younger cohorts.

§ 08

International Comparison

Several countries have addressed comparable structural problems through different labour market models. A common feature is the combination of labour market flexibility with social protection (Andersen & Svarer, 2007).

Country Model Key feature
Denmark Flexicurity Flexibility + secured social protection
Netherlands Extensive part-time employment Work-sharing — low entry threshold
Germany Employer-based training system Matching built into vocational education
Sweden Partial-capacity employment models Intermediate labour markets as institutions

The flexicurity model has attracted the most international attention, but its transferability to Finland is uncertain — the institutional starting conditions differ substantially. Sweden's partial-capacity employment model is likely closer to the Finnish context and may offer more directly applicable lessons.

§ 09

Policy Framework

The analysis supports the identification of several possible interventions. They are not mutually exclusive — they are mutually reinforcing.

# Intervention Target area
1 Recognise structural segmentation as the diagnostic frame for policy Policy knowledge base
2 Closer integration of education institutions and employer networks Matching failure
3 Intermediate labour markets for partial-capacity workers Inclusion
4 Rapid adult training pipelines aligned to shortage sectors Productivity
5 Reduce perceived recruitment risk for employers Employment rate
6 Flexible arrangements for the late career stage Workers aged 55+

Implementation requires coordination across multiple actors: employment authorities, educational institutions, firms, and civil society organisations. No single intervention is sufficient — what is required is a system-level change in the logic of matching.

§ 10

The Cultural Dimension — Heroic Work and Everyday Labour

This section examines the cultural dimension of contemporary labour markets — a perspective that is frequently underrepresented in labour market research despite its material effects.

The shift in work identity. In Finnish — and more broadly Western — work culture, a significant transition has occurred: work is increasingly experienced as a source of identity, vocation, and self-realisation. Work is not undertaken merely for income; it is expected to be meaningful, inspiring, and personally rewarding (Nätti et al., 2021).

Heroic culture and its shadow. This development has produced what might be called a heroic work culture: entrepreneurship, passion, success, and sector-belonging are idealised — not merely competent work performance. Start-up culture, portfolio careers, and personal branding are the symbolic expressions of this tendency.

Heroic culture creates an implicit hierarchy in which "meaningful" work is valued above ordinary, repeatable-skill work. This hierarchy influences recruitment decisions, educational choices, and social recognition.

Effect on workers aged 55 and above. Older workers have generally been formed in a different work culture — one in which work was a value in itself, professional skill accumulated over decades, and employer loyalty was the norm. This cultural gap can negatively affect their market position independently of their actual competence and productivity.

Structural consequence. Heroic culture may partly reinforce the two-segment division: Segment A attracts the "passionate" knowledge worker, while Segment B receives less cultural attention. Societally essential everyday labour — care work, basic industry, logistics — does not receive the cultural recognition it warrants, even as labour shortage in these sectors is acute.

Work is no longer merely performed — it is performed. This performative dimension transforms recruitment markets in ways that place representatives of different generations in unequal positions — not on grounds of competence, but on grounds of narrative.

§ 11

Policy Implications for Ageing Societies

Finland's situation is not an exception but a case study of a broader international phenomenon. Population ageing affects all OECD countries, and the labour market position of workers aged 55 and above is becoming one of the most significant structural policy questions of the coming decade.

Demographic pressure. In most Western countries, replacing the departing generation using only younger cohorts is not possible. OECD assessments indicate that raising the labour force participation of older workers by five percentage points would, in most countries, compensate for one to two years of demographic deficit (OECD, 2023).

The two-segment problem is universal. The same paradox — simultaneous labour shortage and older-worker unemployment — has been documented in Germany, the United Kingdom, Japan, and South Korea. Institutional solutions differ, but the structural problem is recognisable across all cases.

Finland's opportunity. Finland's distinctive advantage is its dense institutional structure: short distances between public authorities, educational institutions, and firms allow rapid policy development. This has been recognised in international comparative assessments. Finland thus has both the need and the institutional capacity to act — the primary constraint is currently one of political will.

International Relevance

The two-segment model developed in this paper is applicable more broadly to ageing Western economies. Finland functions as a laboratory in which structural tensions are especially visible and institutional intervention capacity is exceptionally good. The paper's findings therefore carry potential relevance for international policy discourse.

Conclusion — § 12

The central problem of Finland's labour market is not the number of available positions but the system's capacity to connect people, skills, and positions effectively. People, competence, and jobs exist — but the system does not always combine them efficiently.

For workers aged 55 and above, this matching failure is concrete: experienced professionals can remain outside the labour market while labour shortages persist in the very sectors where their experience is most valuable.

Finland's labour market problem is not a deficit of positions but a deficit in the system's ability to match people, skills, and work effectively. Removing structural and institutional barriers — combined with a broadening of the cultural recognition accorded to everyday labour — could materially improve the functioning of the labour market and simultaneously reduce the social costs of long-term unemployment.
